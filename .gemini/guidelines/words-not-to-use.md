# Words and Phrases to Avoid in Technical Writing

This document defines words, phrases, and patterns commonly overused by AI writers that make content feel generic and artificial. Use natural, specific, concrete language instead.

## ⛔ Banned Words and Phrases

### Overused Intensifiers
- delve/delving (use: explore, examine, investigate, analyze)
- leverage (use: use, apply, utilize when specific)
- robust (use: reliable, stable, well-tested, production-ready)
- seamless/seamlessly (use: smooth, integrated, works well with)
- elevate (use: improve, enhance, increase)
- revolutionize/game-changer (use: improves, changes, advances)
- cutting-edge (use: modern, current, latest)
- state-of-the-art (use: advanced, sophisticated, well-designed)
- world-class (use: high-quality, professional, excellent)
- best-in-class (use: leading, top-tier, high-performing)

### Vague Business Jargon
- synergy (use: combined effect, collaboration, integration)
- paradigm shift (use: major change, fundamental shift)
- disruptive (use: innovative, transformative, significant change)
- holistic (use: comprehensive, complete, thorough)
- streamline (use: simplify, optimize, make efficient)
- empower (use: enable, allow, help, give ability to)
- unlock (use: enable, access, reveal, gain)
- harness (use: use, apply, take advantage of)
- tap into (use: use, access, connect to)
- ecosystem (use: environment, platform, system)

### Purple Prose / Flowery Language
- tapestry (use: combination, mix, collection)
- landscape (in non-literal sense - use: field, area, space, environment)
- journey (use: process, path, progression, experience)
- realm (use: area, field, domain)
- sphere (use: area, field, domain)
- pivotal (use: important, critical, key)
- paramount (use: essential, critical, most important)
- plethora (use: many, numerous, several)
- myriad (use: many, numerous, various)
- cornerstone (use: foundation, basis, key element)

### Clichéd Transitions and Connectors
- it's worth noting that (just state the fact)
- it goes without saying (if it goes without saying, don't say it)
- at the end of the day (use: ultimately, finally, in conclusion)
- when all is said and done (use: ultimately, in the end)
- in today's fast-paced world (remove or be specific: "in 2025")
- in this day and age (use: currently, now, today)
- needless to say (if needless, don't say it)
- suffice it to say (just say it directly)
- the fact of the matter is (just state the fact)

### Overused Opening Phrases
- "In the realm of..." (just start with the subject)
- "In the world of..." (just start with the subject)
- "When it comes to..." (just start with the subject)
- "It's important to note that..." (just state it)
- "One of the key aspects/factors is..." (just state it)
- "In essence..." (just state it directly)
- "Essentially..." (use: basically, fundamentally, or remove)
- "Arguably..." (just make your point)
- "Indeed..." (remove or use: in fact, actually)

### Vague Qualifiers
- quite (usually unnecessary - remove or be specific)
- very (be more specific instead - "very fast" → "rapid")
- really (usually unnecessary - remove)
- extremely (be specific about degree)
- highly (be specific - "highly effective" → "90% success rate")
- significantly (provide numbers if possible)
- considerably (provide numbers if possible)
- substantially (provide numbers if possible)

### Passive Voice Red Flags
- "can be seen as" (use: is, appears, seems)
- "it should be noted" (just state it)
- "it is important to understand" (just explain it)
- "it has been shown" (use: studies show, research indicates)
- "it is widely recognized" (use: experts agree, research shows)

### Meta-Commentary (Writing About Writing)
- "let's dive into..." (just start the topic)
- "let's explore..." (just explore it)
- "let's take a look at..." (just look at it)
- "we'll examine..." (just examine it)
- "in this article, we will discuss..." (just discuss it)
- "first, we'll cover..." (just cover it)

### Overused Metaphors
- tip of the iceberg (use: small part, beginning)
- low-hanging fruit (use: easy wins, quick improvements)
- move the needle (use: make progress, show results)
- drinking from a fire hose (use: overwhelming amount of information)
- boil the ocean (use: overly ambitious, unrealistic scope)

### AI Writing Tell-Tale Signs
- "in conclusion, [topic] is important because..." (varied conclusions)
- Starting every paragraph with "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally"
- Ending articles with "In summary, [restate everything]"
- "As we've seen..." (just reference specific points)
- "[Topic] is not just about X, it's also about Y" (overused pattern)

## ✅ Better Alternatives and Patterns

### Instead of Vague Claims, Use Specific Evidence
❌ "This significantly improves performance"
✅ "This reduces build time from 10 minutes to 2 minutes"

❌ "The tool is highly scalable"
✅ "The tool handles 10,000 requests per second"

❌ "This provides robust security"
✅ "This uses AES-256 encryption and OAuth 2.0 authentication"

### Instead of Flowery Transitions, Use Direct Statement
❌ "In the realm of cloud computing, it's worth noting that..."
✅ "Azure provides three deployment models..."

❌ "When it comes to PowerShell automation, one of the key aspects is..."
✅ "PowerShell automation requires error handling..."

❌ "At the end of the day, performance optimization is critical"
✅ "Performance optimization reduces costs and improves user experience"

### Instead of Vague Descriptions, Use Concrete Examples
❌ "This approach leverages cutting-edge technology to streamline workflows"
✅ "This approach uses GitHub Actions to automate deployment in 3 steps"

❌ "The solution provides a seamless experience"
✅ "Users can deploy with a single command: `git push`"

### Instead of Meta-Commentary, Just Do It
❌ "Let's dive into the various configuration options available"
✅ "Configuration options include..."

❌ "In this section, we'll explore error handling"
✅ "Error handling requires try-catch blocks"

## Writing Style Principles

### 1. Be Specific Over General
- Use concrete numbers, not ranges or approximations
- Name specific tools, not categories
- Show actual code, not descriptions of code

### 2. Be Direct Over Diplomatic
- State facts clearly without hedging
- Use active voice predominantly
- Remove unnecessary qualifiers

### 3. Be Technical Over Buzzwords
- Use precise technical terms
- Avoid marketing language
- Choose clarity over cleverness

### 4. Be Practical Over Theoretical
- Lead with working examples
- Show real-world scenarios
- Provide actionable steps

### 5. Be Concise Over Verbose
- Remove filler phrases
- Cut redundant explanations
- Get to the point quickly

## Quick Self-Check Questions

Before finalizing content, ask:
1. Could this sentence appear in marketing copy? (If yes, rewrite)
2. Would a human naturally say this? (If no, rewrite)
3. Does this include specific, verifiable information? (If no, add it)
4. Can I remove words without losing meaning? (If yes, remove them)
5. Am I showing AND telling? (Show through examples, don't just tell)

## Enforcement

These guidelines apply to ALL generated content:
- Article outlines
- Full articles
- Code comments
- Documentation
- Command descriptions

When generating content, actively avoid these patterns. If you catch yourself using these phrases, immediately rewrite with specific, direct alternatives.
